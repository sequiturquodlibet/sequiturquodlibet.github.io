<h2 id="3"><i>The Member of the Wedding</i> (Carson McCullers, 1946)<br><sub>February 26, 2026</sub></h2>

Carson McCullers, best known for <i>The Heart is a Lonely Hunter</i>, seems to have gone out of fashion. Her nondescript style and unassuming plotlines resist the sort of politically-infused literary criticism that is now <i>de rigueur</i>. Perhaps that is McCullers' strength: she never betrays any preachiness. The narration and dialogue are sincere to a fault, and if to the modern reader they come across as sentimental, that only reflects the cynicism of our time.

<i>The Member of the Wedding's</i> protagonist is Frankie, a twelve-year-old tomboy who develops a monomania for her older brother's upcoming wedding. She is determined to join the honeymoon as a third wheel, travelling the world in grand, swashbuckling style as a sidekick to husband and wife. Such naïvete would be rare in today's tweens. But Frankie still lives in the age of soda pop stands and organ grinders with little monkeys on their backs; so we buy the premise, even if it has the air of a fairy tale.

The briefest sketch of the plot will suffice: Frankie runs around her dreary Southern hometown preparing for the wedding, getting her social affairs in order, all with the expectation that she will never return. The big day comes and goes; the newlyweds drive off into the sunset without her. She returns home devastated. This isn't really a spoiler, because it is telegraphed so far in advance. The only person who is shocked is Frankie herself.

These ought to be the ingredients for a girly melodrama. Instead, we get a study in loneliness (a McCullers staple).

Frankie has a simple want: to be included in something that matters, anything. The wedding just so happens to be the nearest prospect. She also doesn't like herself very much, and is old enough to understand that if you don't like yourself, others won't either. Her solution is: become someone else. She renames herself "F. Jasmine," buys a new dress, and manufactures a different personality with new, "grown-up" opinions and attitudes to Frankie's familiar little world. The third-person narrator takes this overnight transformation seriously, yet nobody else does, least of all the two characters closest to Frankie: the aging maid and the six-year-old boy down the street. The maid thinks Frankie has gone insane and the boy is too simple-minded to perceive much of a change at all.

In town she meets a soldier out on leave. He mistakes her for a much older girl and invites her on a date. Here, finally, is F. Jasmine's opportunity to soothe her lonesomeness, and more importantly to assert an existence. Not solipsistically, but to others &mdash; which, as she's figured out, is the only kind of existence that carries weight. It all comes out wrong. The things F. Jasmine says and does still resemble the things Frankie would say and do; outside of that, there is little to say or do at all. F. Jasmine <i>qua</i> F. Jasmine is an empty signifier; yes, she <i>could</i> be anyone, but such potentiality is liberating only in the same unhelpful way as retrograde amnesia.

Following her humiliation at the wedding, F. Jasmine undergoes a final transformation; she is now Frances, Frankie's real name. The coming-of-age symbolism here is a bit transparent. What saves it from a cliché is that the narrator, once again, accepts the substitution at face value, so we do, too.

McCullers isn't big on didactics, but if there is a moral to this story, it goes something like this: Your future is constrained by your past, and your identity belongs as much to others as it does to you. Which isn't an empowering or popular sentiment, but which, maybe, is also true.

<h2 id="2">A Supposedly Fun Thing I'll Never Do Again<sup>1</sup> (David Foster Wallace, 1996)<br><sub>February 24, 2026</sub></h2>

I have a hard time watching David Foster Wallace's interviews. It's not because Wallace is a bad speaker, or because he says the wrong things, or even because of his tragic fate. Rather, it's because Wallace is so transparently, involuntarily earnest that it makes me feel indecent, as though I'm seeing a part of his private inner world I was never supposed to. I wonder if the maximalism of <i>The Broom of the System</i> and <i>Infinite Jest</i> was, in part, Wallace's subconscious attempt to compensate for this transparency; to slide a couple of verbose layers of privacy between himself and his audience.

"A Supposedly Fun Thing" denies this technique to Wallace by imposing a hard word limit. The result is an excellent essay that showcases his signature voice without overstaying its welcome &mdash; while also turning the reader into a bit of a psychological voyeur. The premise is straightforward enough: an introspective landlubber goes on a Caribbean cruise with a bunch of well-adjusted retirees and can't bring himself to have a good time. (The ship is aptly named the <i>Nadir</i>.) Occasionally, accidental humor ensues; more frequently, there are meditations on the fakeness of the hospitality industry and the petty neuroticism that arises when your every whim is catered to. Substitute a few technological references, and the essay could have easily been published half a century earlier, or today. Maybe Wallace would say the cruise ship lifestyle is so contrived that it exists outside of time.

But all this is just a convenient excuse to talk about something more unsettling. The essay's real interest lies in the conflict between the self as a subject, and the self as an object. Wallace intuits from the advertising that the fundamental appeal of a luxury cruise is not so much in its creature comforts, but in the temporary reprieve from quotidian responsibilities and decision-making...

<blockquote><i>"'My [the ad narrator's] toughest decisions had been whether to catch the afternoon showing of <i>Mrs. Doubtfire</i> or play bingo'"</i></blockquote>

...and in fact a reprieve from how you <i>perceive</i> yourself, as your perceptions will be given to you in the declarative second-person...

<blockquote><i>"As you drift along like a cloud on water, the weight of everyday life is magically lifted away, and you seem to be floating on a sea of smiles"</i></blockquote>

...or, in existentialist terms: on this cruise, you won't even have to bear the burden of existing a subject. You can be like the lilies that neither toil nor spin. You can exist in bliss as an unreflective, immanent object.

To treat a person as an object, is normally to demean him, to treat him in some sense as a social inferior. In therapy-speak, one talks of "objectification," "being used," "not seeing the individual," etc. However, a curious inversion occurs when one person waits upon another. The person being waited upon, in the passive voice (the customer), is having the action done to him; he is an object. Yet, in the context of the interaction, the customer is the social <i>superior</i> of the person who is doing the waiting upon (the employee). How could this be? The answer is simply that the employee is <i>also</i> an object. In the ideal customer service interaction, <i>there is no real subject</i>. The customer exists merely to be waited upon (and, it goes without saying, to spend his money); the employee exists merely to pamper the customer, as dictated by the employer, who is perhaps the actual subject somewhere offstage. The difference between the employee's and customer's object status is mostly in that the customer's is recreational, while the employee's is drudgery.

Occasionally, however, one party will do something that spoils the illusion. This is known in the industry as "poor customer service."

On the first day aboard, Wallace spots his suitcase in a big heap of luggage and decides to carry it up to his room himself instead of waiting for the nearby porter. He has made the mistake of asserting his subjecthood. This puts the porter in a bind: if a vacationer is seen carrying his own luggage, it means the porter has failed to do his job. So now the porter has to break character and assert his <i>own</i> subjecthood, if only to convince Wallace to set the suitcase down so they can both return to their roles as objects. This little bout of intersubjectivity causes the whole Luxury Experience to short-circuit, and Wallace later finds himself half-apologizing to a manager for his faux pas. What Wallace is really apologizing for, however, is for "existing" in the etymological Latinate meaning of the word, viz. <i>ex + sistere</i>, standing out.

If we take the etymology a step further, then the opposite of standing out is non-existence... or death, at least metaphorically. Wallace associates cruise liners with death in other ways too: the geriatric clientele; the lethal vastness of the ocean; the corrosive and unceasing effect of salt water on the ship's hull. Little wonder then, that Wallace's strongest emotion on board the <i>Nadir</i> is despair.

There is resignation, too. Over the course of the cruise, Wallace-as-subject is gradually worn down and exhausted by the relentless machinery of hospitality, which is determined not to allow anyone to stand outside of it. Wallace-as-object is victorious, perching on the sundeck, waiting impatiently for the towel boy to fetch him another fluffy piece of linen and wondering if it's time for the sixth meal of the day. One must imagine him happy.

<h2 id="1"><i>Babbitt</i> (Sinclair Lewis, 1922)<br><sub>February 20, 2026</sub></h2>

Fiction is an imperfect vehicle for social criticism. It contains the tension between propaganda &mdash; in the neutral, original sense of the term &mdash; and art, or at least good art. Good art, I submit, must be able to be contemplated as an end in itself. The smoothest, most technically adept propaganda (whether it be <i>Triumph des Willens</i> or a Super Bowl ad) is not good art, because its value is inescapably instrumental. It is so aware of the viewer's gaze, of the Sartrean Other, that it panders. It gives an odor of duplicity, of <i>mauvaise foi</i>.

All this poses a problem for the satirist. He cannot make his criticism directly, if he is to retain a claim to literary merit &mdash; and the merit is important, because that is what elevates the criticism. Otherwise he would write an op-ed. The satirist must conceal his opinions within the framework of his fictional world, using a range of techniques (allusion, allegory, irony, etc.) to nudge the reader into making the correct inferences to the real world. Conceal the opinions too poorly, and the reader sees the soapbox; conceal them too well, and the whole point is lost. The best social criticism in fiction is perhaps that which seems to leak out spontaneously, somewhere beneath the surface of plot and narration; not because of, but despite the narrator's best efforts.

Sinclair Lewis' <i>Babbitt</i> is really two novels: <i>Babbitt</i>  the satire, and <i>Babbitt</i> the character study.

The satire is not good art, and is not even very good satire. Like Lewis' other novels, <i>Babbitt</i>  is set in the fictional Midwestern city of Zenith, and for a few hundred pages the setting is the story's most interesting feature. The prose is ponderous by today's standards and the dialogue is copious but superficial. Several chapters could have been omitted without much loss. There is much mockery of contemporary pieties &mdash; being an Upstanding Citizen, belonging to the Right Clubs, being invited into a Respectable Church, etc. &mdash; that is now more of historical than literary interest. The cast of supporting characters is huge, but quickly blurs together, and they differ from the eponymous protagonist mostly in that the reader is spared their internal monologues. This is Lewis' way of telling us that the pretenders to 1920s American high society are empty vessels. But he could have said as much in an op-ed and saved us the trouble.

The character study is good art, when it finally appears in the last third of the book. Here we see George F. Babbitt not as a punching-bag for Lewis' wit, but as a man with a relatable and dreadful affliction: try as he might, he cannot escape himself. He is in fact everything Lewis has hammered home: a cipher, a zero, incapable of a thought or opinion not contingent on another's approval. What's more, Babbitt realizes this for himself; and in so doing, gains a level of self-awareness not afforded to anyone else in the story. Yet this development offers no relief. He cannot acquire an essence when he had none before, and there is no one who could understand or share his plight. The banal strictures of Zenith society (which the narrator holds in such contempt) are what give Babbitt's life meaning. The only thing Babbitt can do with self-awareness and freedom is to run away from them.

Now this is a glimpse into the human condition worthy of Camus or Dostoevsky. The only problem is, it's not satire.

There is a deeper aesthetic flaw in <i>Babbitt</i>, which we can better assess a century in retrospect. The fact is that satire is ultimately a tool of destruction. The satirist can tear down a structure, but he cannot build a new one. In reading Lewis' interminable descriptions of dinner parties, of stuffy private clubs and nosy neighbors and fair-weather friends, we catch a glimpse of an America that is so quaint and naïve that it strains credulity circa 2026. George F. Babbitt's world was the size of Zenith; ours has shrunk to the size of a smartphone. Like Babbitt, we are also ciphers; unlike Babbitt, there is no longer a Good Citizens' League in whose confines we could seek metaphysical refuge. We have to make do with scrolling on social media as we stand in the chilly outdoors.
